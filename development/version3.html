In California, 93% of foster youth aspire to attend college, yet only 4% graduate with a bachelor's degree compared to 50% of their non-foster peers. Those from foster care who do attend college face numerous challenges, including financial constraints, lack of family support, academic underpreparedness, mental health struggles, and the absence of emotional support from caring adults.

The foster care system in California plays a crucial role in protecting vulnerable children from harm. Its primary goals include ensuring children's safety, protecting them from maltreatment and neglect, placing them in family-like settings, and providing families with the support needed for children to safely return home whenever possible.
However, the reasons for entry into the foster care system are multiple, complex, and often intertwined with the social and environmental challenges associated with poverty. These experiences significantly impact the educational outcomes of foster youth, such as multiple school moves and the disruption of community ties.
Students in foster care often face complex challenges arising from the instability of their living arrangements. Although children in foster care have the right to remain in their schools of origin, removal from the family home or changes in foster care placement can cause students to move schools or even districts. This instability can interrupt students' learning progression, causing them to miss topics or material already covered at their new school and adjust to new curricula, teachers, and social environments.

High-Poverty Schools: Nearly half of all students in foster care are enrolled in the highest-poverty schools, which tend to have greater resourcing challenges.
Absenteeism and Suspension: Foster youth are twice as likely to be chronically absent and more than four times as likely to be suspended as their non-foster peers.
Academic Achievement: On state tests, only 24% of foster youth meet proficiency standards in English language arts, and 15% in mathematics, compared to 51% and 40% of non-foster students, respectively.
